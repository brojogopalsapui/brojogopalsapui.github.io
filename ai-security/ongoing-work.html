Research Watch

Highlighted ongoing directions

Research Watch Agentic AI • 2025–2026 signal

Why agentic AI changes the security problem

Security is no longer only about whether a model produces a harmful answer. Once the system can plan, keep state, read untrusted content, call tools, and act over multiple steps, the real problem becomes goal integrity under autonomy.

That shift is important because agentic systems do not fail in the same way as one-shot assistants. A normal chatbot may produce a bad answer, but a browser agent, coding agent, inbox agent, or workflow agent can convert that mistake into state changes, file access, data transfer, purchases, approvals, or multi-step action sequences. The attack surface therefore moves from isolated prompt safety to a larger loop involving memory, delegation, tool permissions, identity propagation, and recovery after partial compromise.

Recent guidance is converging on the same conclusion from different directions. OWASP now treats agentic applications as a distinct security class rather than a simple extension of LLM apps, and NIST's ongoing control-overlay work explicitly separates single-agent and multi-agent use cases. Industry guidance on prompt injection is also moving away from the idea of a perfect filter and toward architectural containment: assume some malicious instructions will get through, then design the system so they cannot easily trigger high-impact actions. In other words, the main question is no longer “can the model be tricked?” but “what is the maximum damage if it is tricked for one step, five steps, or fifty?”.

What is changing technically

  • Prompt injection becomes more dangerous when the model can browse, retrieve, and execute actions.
  • Memory turns one-time manipulation into persistence if poisoned notes, summaries, or preferences survive into future tasks.
  • Delegation and multi-agent composition introduce new trust boundaries between planner, executor, retriever, and sub-agent roles.
  • Approval becomes part of the security architecture, not just a UX feature.

What reviewers should notice

  • Whether the paper or system defines explicit autonomy boundaries rather than only model safety prompts.
  • Whether tool permissions are scoped per task, per identity, and per action type.
  • Whether memory is treated as a governed state object or just a convenient persistence layer.
  • Whether evaluation includes long-horizon and indirect attacks, not only single-turn jailbreaks.
Current research tension: the field wants useful autonomy, but every increase in autonomy expands the blast radius of partial compromise. This is why the strongest current work is shifting toward capability bounding, authenticated workflows, explicit human approval for high-impact actions, and runtime tracing that makes agent behavior inspectable rather than magically autonomous.

Research Watch Edge AI • implementation reality

Edge AI makes implementation details matter

On-device deployment shifts the problem from purely software trust to physical, architectural, and side-channel-aware trust. Once the model is local, the attacker is no longer only querying an API; they may probe the board, observe memory traffic, tamper with firmware, spoof sensors, or exploit the update path.

This is why edge AI is not simply “cloud AI moved closer to the sensor.” The trust assumptions change at multiple levels. The device may be intermittently connected, power constrained, physically reachable, and expected to operate in hostile or uncontrolled environments. That means secure boot, hardware-backed identity, attestation, provisioning, rollback protection, OTA update integrity, protected model storage, and debug-port policy become central. Even the same model can look different from a security perspective after quantization, pruning, accelerator mapping, or local caching because those choices change leakage surfaces and runtime behavior.

Current device-security guidance increasingly emphasizes lifecycle assurance, not only runtime hardening. Trusted onboarding, identity binding, attestation tokens, and field lifecycle management are now treated as part of the operational security boundary. This is especially relevant for AI devices because a local compromise can expose not just the firmware and keys, but also model parameters, user data, embeddings, sensor traces, and downstream control logic. As edge inference expands, “implementation details” are no longer engineering footnotes; they are part of the threat model itself.

What is changing technically

  • Model confidentiality becomes a local storage and in-memory execution problem.
  • Secure boot and attestation now matter for model trust, not only operating system trust.
  • Sensor validity and firmware lifecycle have to be discussed together.
  • Resource limits force lightweight security, so cloud-style defense stacks often do not translate directly.

What reviewers should notice

  • Whether the work defines a real device-level attacker and not just a software-only adversary.
  • Whether provisioning, update, and recovery are part of the claimed security story.
  • Whether hardware mapping choices such as quantization or accelerator scheduling alter leakage or FI exposure.
  • Whether the paper explains how security scales across a fleet, not only on one prototype board.
Current research tension: edge devices are expected to be private, fast, and low-power at the same time. But strong protection for storage, runtime state, sensor integrity, and update governance always consumes budget. This is why edge security increasingly rewards co-design across firmware, device architecture, model compression, and fleet management rather than one-layer fixes.

Research Watch Physical AI • embodied systems

Physical AI needs security and safety together

When AI senses and acts in the world, latency, timing, reliability, security, and safety stop being separate checkboxes. They become tightly coupled design goals across the full perception-to-action loop.

Physical AI is broader than robotics hype. It includes the convergence of AI with embodied or cyber-physical platforms such as robots, autonomous vehicles, drones, industrial automation, and other systems where perception, planning, communication, and actuation are linked. In these systems, security incidents do not remain abstract. A manipulated sensor stream, delayed control message, corrupted state estimate, or planner-controller mismatch can change physical behavior. That is why the evaluation lens must expand from “did the model output the wrong class?” to “how did the full system behave under uncertainty, disturbance, latency, or attack?”.

Recent policy and operational guidance also points in this direction. Physical AI is increasingly being discussed as the convergence of AI and robotics, while secure integration guidance for AI in operational technology emphasizes that availability, safety, and control integrity must remain primary even when AI is added. This creates a research demand for cross-layer evaluation: secure sensing, trusted compute, authenticated communication, bounded actuation, runtime anomaly detection, and safe fallback behavior all need to be analyzed together. In short, physical AI security cannot be reduced to adversarial examples or to firmware protection alone. It is a systems problem where cyber compromise and unsafe physical behavior can become the same event.

What is changing technically

  • Perception, control, communication, and actuation must be evaluated as one chain rather than separate modules.
  • Timing and delay become security-relevant because stale or manipulated state can still drive action.
  • Fail-safe degradation matters as much as attack prevention because some disturbances will always occur in the physical world.
  • Sensor fusion, estimator trust, and controller bounds are becoming central research topics.

What reviewers should notice

  • Whether the work links model failure to system-level physical consequence.
  • Whether safety fallback and bounded control are explicitly discussed.
  • Whether communication delays, localization drift, spoofing, or jamming are part of the evaluation assumptions.
  • Whether the paper distinguishes cyber robustness from true operational resilience.
Current research tension: physical AI promises more autonomy in the real world, but the real world is messy, delayed, noisy, and adversarial. The strongest upcoming work will likely be the work that links secure perception, trustworthy control, runtime monitoring, and graceful fail-safe behavior, rather than optimizing any one module in isolation.
